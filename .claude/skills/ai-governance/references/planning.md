# Planning

## Addressing Risks and Opportunities

**Purpose:** Proactively identify what could go wrong and what could go right, then plan for both.

Start by looking at the full picture — organizational context, interested parties, the AI system's intended use — and determine the risks and opportunities that matter. Maintain a risk register with clear criteria for what's acceptable and what isn't.

For RecoveryLM, risks span technical (model hallucination, crisis detection failures), ethical (bias in responses, over-reliance replacing professional care), privacy (exposure of recovery status), and societal (reinforcing stigma, inequitable access).

## Risk Assessment

**Purpose:** A repeatable, consistent process for identifying and prioritizing AI risks.

The risk assessment process should align with our policy and objectives. For each risk: identify it, analyze its potential impact on the organization, individuals, groups, and society, assess how likely it is, determine its severity level, and prioritize it for treatment.

For RecoveryLM, crisis detection accuracy is a top-tier risk — a missed suicidal ideation signal has catastrophic consequences. Model drift affecting response quality is another. These need different assessment approaches than, say, a UI bug in a widget.

## Risk Treatment

**Purpose:** Decide what to do about each unacceptable risk, then do it.

For each risk that exceeds acceptable levels, select a treatment: avoid it, mitigate it with controls, transfer it, or accept it with justification. Determine what controls are needed, check which existing controls already help, identify gaps, and produce a treatment plan. Document what's been decided and why — including which risks are accepted and by whom.

## Impact Assessment

**Purpose:** Understand the real-world consequences of our AI system on people and society.

Go beyond organizational risk to assess what happens to actual humans. Consider intended use and foreseeable misuse. For RecoveryLM: What happens if someone in crisis gets a dismissive response? What if recovery status is exposed to an employer? What if the system reinforces harmful stereotypes about addiction? These consequences should feed directly back into risk assessment.

## Objectives

**Purpose:** Turn policy commitments into measurable goals.

Objectives should be consistent with the policy, measurable where practical, tracked over time, and updated as things change. For each objective, define: what will be done, what resources are needed, who owns it, when it should be completed, and how success will be evaluated.

## Planning Changes

**Purpose:** When the governance system itself needs to change, do it deliberately — not ad hoc.

Any changes to the management system should be planned, with consideration for the purpose of the change, potential consequences, resource needs, and impact on existing responsibilities and controls.
